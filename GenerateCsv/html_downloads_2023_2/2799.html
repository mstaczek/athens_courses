Objectives:

The objectives of this course are to give to students a theoretical and practical understanding of the design of flight control systems for aerospace applications. Topics covered will range from system identification, robust/nonlinear/intelligent control and control allocation for a variety of aerospace vehicles (drones, aircraft and spacecraft).

Programme to be followed:

Monday 20/11 (Instructor: prof. Coen de Visser)- Afternoon from13:00 oclcok

The objective of this part is to cover theory and tools for system identification of multirotor drones. In the first part, a refresher on aircraft state reconstruction and parameter estimation techniques, with a focus on the two-step method (grey box) and data-driven (black box) methods will be done. Multivariate splines will be introduced as a more advanced model structure, with accompanying least squares estimators. A system identification pipeline in Python that spans the process from hardware parameters (moments of inertia, mass, etc.) to design of flight experiments, execution of flight tests, state reconstruction, model structure definition, model parameter estimation, and finally model validation, will be introduced. Particular attention will be on obtaining models of the control effectiveness of the multirotors. In the second part, flight experiments will be conducted (automatically, and/or manually) in the TU-Delft CyberZoo using a number of different multirotor drones, after which the system identification pipeline will be applied to the flight data.

 

Tuesday 21/11(Instructor: prof. Spilios Theodoulis)

The objective of this part is to cover the theoretical tools and underpinnings of robust H-infinity control from a practical point of view. The concept of singular values will be introduced and its use for the control of multivariable systems will be highlighted. The concept of loop shaping both in open and closed loop will be detailed and this will lead to design objectives to be met by the robust controller. The design and analysis of such controllers in an H-infinity framework will be addressed both for full order or reduced order controllers (e.g. PID). A MATLAB/Simulink demo will be then performed live and together with the students in order to showcase the theory introduced by the use of a highly maneuverable, open-loop unstable aerospace vehicle.

 

Wednesday 22/2 (Instructor: prof. Ewoud Smeur)

In the first part of this lecture, several control allocation methods, starting from unconstrained Control Allocation using the pseudo-inverse will be discussed. More advanced control allocation schemes that apply to linearized plants such as direct control allocation and optimization schemes using quadratic programming will be also covered. Finally control allocation in the case of a very non-linear plant will be presented. In the second part of the lecture, a quadcopter with some of the control allocation methods will be programmed and test-flied it in the CyberZoo to observe the differences between the methods.

 

Thursday 23/2 (Instructor: prof. Erwin Mooij and prof. Xuerui Wang)

In the first part of this lecture the modelling of a space launcher, flexibility and sloshing effect will be presented and followed by the stability analysis of a flexible launcher with and without sloshing. Filtering of the forward and feedback signals and the effect of engine dynamics will be also added. The robust control of this system namely PD, INDI, and the combination of sliding modes/INDI control will be illustrated and finally the effect of slosh feedback, linked with a slosh observer will be shown. If time permits, a slot on simple adaptive control applied to winged entry vehicles will be also added.

In the second part of this lecture the world’s first translation-induced camber morphing wing smart-x will be demonstrated. Videos will be shown about the design principles and technology innovations involved in this project. Finally, TU Delft experimental Flying-V aircraft will be showcased together with research highlights and various running research projects.

 

Friday 24/2 (Instructor: prof. Erik-Jan van Kampen)

The first part of this lecture will cover Artificial Neural Networks (ANN) presenting an introduction, a viewing of neural networks from a non-mathematical perspective, the coupling of visual perspective to a mathematical description, off-line training, (re-)definition of the neural network based on training performance, on-line training and finally advanced neural network structures and training algorithms.

The second part of this lecture will focus on Reinforcement Learning (RL) for flight control presenting also an introduction, discrete RL, Monte Carlo vs TD, continuous RL, RL applied to an F-16 and  also current work & remaining challenges.

This part will be mainly in the form of a lecture, but there will be some interactive elements in there, where students have to work out some simple problems on worksheets that are handed out. Some MATLAB/Simulink demonstrations of ANN and RL will be also shown.
